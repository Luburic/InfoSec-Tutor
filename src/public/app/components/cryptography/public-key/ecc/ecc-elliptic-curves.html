Ok, so, what exactly are elliptic curves? Well, put simply, an elliptic curve is a set of points described by the equation:

$y^2 = x^3 + ax + b$(ECC A 1)

where $4a^3 + 27b^2 \ne 0$, in order to avoid singular curves. What are singular curves you might ask, well, apparently, they are elliptic curves where $4a^3 + 27b^2 = 0$. Not funny? No, it's not. That being said, in mathematics a singularity is in general a point at which a given mathematical object is not defined, or a point of an exceptional set where it fails to be well-behaved in some particular way. Take dividing by zero as an example. In short, singular curves are bad, m'kay?

However, for our understanding of ECC, a set of points described by the equation ECC A 1 will suffice. Let's take a look at some pictures at fig. 1.

Depending on the values of the domain parameters ($a, b$) the elliptic curves assume different shapes on the plane as can be seen above. By the way, did you notice the symmetry about the $x$-axis? This, of course, is no surprise, as $y$ in ECC A 1 is squared. Now, we also need a point at infinity, which we'll denote as 0 (zero), to be part of our curve. Taking all of this into account we can refine our definition of elliptic curves as follows:

$\left\{ (x, y) \in \mathbb{R}^2\ |\ y^2 = x^3 + ax + b,\ 4 a^3 + 27 b^2 \ne 0 \right\}\ \cup\ \left\{ 0 \right\}$(ECC A 2)
